# Main Results

This page presents the key results from optimization of aviation decarbonization scenarios.

## Baseline Scenarios

The baseline scenarios show no-policy projections under different SSP narratives (SSP1-1.9, SSP2-2.6, SSP5-4.5).

### Key Findings

1. **Emissions Peak Around 2040**: All baseline scenarios show emissions peaking around 2040, where efficiency gains from fleet replacement begin to outpace demand growth

2. **Demand Drives Overall Trends**:
   - SSP5 shows highest initial growth
   - SSP1 and SSP2 are similar until ~2050
   - SSP2 continues growing while SSP1 peaks and declines
   - SSP5 shows sustained high demand

3. **Fleet Renewal Impact Varies by Market**:
   - **General aviation** (3% of supply): >3x efficiency gain possible, but long lifetimes delay impact
   - **Commuter** (22% of supply): Moderate efficiency gains, fast renewal, high system-wide impact
   - **Regional** (39% of supply): Similar to commuter, drives most system-wide improvements
   - **Short-medium** (21% of supply): Slower renewal, efficiency gains delayed
   - **Long-range** (15% of supply): Slowest renewal, requires mature technology for improvements

4. **Emissions Remain High**: By 2070:
   - SSP1: ~50% above 2019 levels
   - SSP2: ~80% above 2019 levels
   - SSP5: ~120% above 2019 levels

5. **Carbon Budget Exceedance**: All scenarios exceed their fair share of Paris Agreement carbon budget:
   - Factor of 2-3x over allocated budget
   - SSP5 exceeds by 2040
   - SSP1 and SSP2 exceed by 2046

**Conclusion**: Baseline scenarios without mitigation policies are incompatible with climate targets.

## Drop-in Mitigation Scenarios

Drop-in scenarios introduce Sustainable Aviation Fuels (SAF) - biofuels and electrofuels - while keeping conventional aircraft.

### Scenario Variants

**Trend**: 5% of global biomass/electricity allocated to aviation
**Availability**: 8.6% of global biomass/electricity allocated to aviation
**Low-demand**: 5% allocation + demand management to meet carbon budget

### Key Findings

1. **Limited Technology Sensitivity**: Drop-in scenarios show less sensitivity to aircraft technology assumptions compared to breakthrough scenarios

2. **SAF Blend Evolution**:
   - Early period: Dominated by fossil kerosene
   - Mid-term: Increasing biofuel share (HEFA pathway preferred initially)
   - Late-term: Electrofuels increase as electricity decarbonizes
   - Pathway choice depends on resource availability and carbon intensity

3. **Trend Variant** (5% allocation):
   - Emissions reach ~2019 levels by 2070
   - Fails to phase out fossil fuels
   - Exceeds carbon budget
   - Limited by biomass and electricity availability

4. **Availability Variant** (8.6% allocation):
   - Further emission reductions possible
   - Much lower fossil consumption
   - Still exceeds carbon budget
   - Demonstrates resource constraint as key limiting factor

5. **Low-Demand Variant** (5% allocation + demand caps):
   - Traffic ~50% of trend by 2070 (still 50% above 2019)
   - Meets Paris Agreement carbon budget
   - Phases out fossil consumption
   - Lower emissions peak
   - Demonstrates necessity of demand management with limited resources

### Biofuel Pathway Selection

**HEFA (Hydroprocessed Esters and Fatty Acids)**:
- Lower biomass consumption (1.95 MJ/MJ vs 5.0 for FT)
- Higher emissions (62.73 g CO2/MJ vs 35.3 for FT)
- Preferred when fossil consumption is still high

**FT (Fischer-Tropsch)**:
- Higher biomass consumption
- Lower emissions
- Becomes preferred as fossil phase-out progresses

**Electrofuels (Power-to-Liquid)**:
- Depends on electricity grid carbon intensity
- Increases share as grid decarbonizes
- Competes with alternative aircraft for electricity in Breakthrough scenarios

### Energy System Implications

The energy sankey diagrams show:
- Multiple competing pathways for limited resources
- Trade-offs between biofuel pathways
- Increasing role of electricity as grid decarbonizes
- Importance of lifecycle emissions accounting

## Breakthrough Mitigation Scenarios

Breakthrough scenarios add alternative aircraft architectures: battery-electric, liquid hydrogen fuel cell, and liquid hydrogen gas turbine.

### Scenario Variants

Same structure as Drop-in: trend, availability, and low-demand variants with technology scenarios.

### Key Findings

1. **High Technology Sensitivity**: Results vary dramatically with technology assumptions:
   - **Lower tech**: Higher emissions, earlier alternative aircraft deployment needed
   - **Upper tech**: Lower emissions, can delay alternative aircraft deployment

2. **Trend Variant** (5% allocation):
   - **Lower tech**: Emissions still exceed 2019 levels, some fossil consumption remains
   - **Upper tech**: Emissions less than half of 2019, near-zero fossil by 2070
   - Alternative aircraft crucial for long-term deep decarbonization

3. **Availability Variant** (8.6% allocation):
   - Can achieve near-zero emissions post-2070
   - Fossil phase-out as early as 2060
   - Relaxes biomass constraint (less biofuel needed)
   - Still exceeds cumulative carbon budget

4. **Low-Demand Variant** (5% allocation + demand caps):
   - Much easier to meet carbon budget compared to Drop-in low-demand
   - Traffic up to 140% above 2019 possible (vs 200% trend)
   - Demonstrates complementarity of alternative aircraft and demand management

### Fleet Composition by Market

**General Aviation** (shortest range):
- First to adopt new technologies
- Battery-electric becomes dominant with Upper tech
- Shows highest sensitivity to technology assumptions

**Commuter and Regional**:
- Second wave of alternative aircraft adoption
- Mix of hydrogen fuel cell and gas turbine
- Technology choice depends on maturity assumptions

**Short-Medium and Long-Range**:
- Last to transition
- Remain dependent on drop-in fuels longer
- Hydrogen gas turbine becomes viable with Upper tech
- Range and payload constraints more binding

### Technology Choice Drivers

**Battery-Electric**:
- Limited to shortest ranges (general, possibly commuter)
- Requires high battery specific energy (>600 Wh/kg)
- Zero emissions, but high weight penalty limits range

**Hydrogen Fuel Cell**:
- Suitable for short to medium ranges
- Requires mature fuel cell technology (>3 kW/kg, >50% efficiency)
- Lower emissions than gas turbine, but heavier system

**Hydrogen Gas Turbine**:
- Can serve all markets including long-range
- Similar performance to conventional kerosene turbine
- Requires cryogenic tank technology (>35% gravimetric index)
- Depends on low-carbon hydrogen availability

### Energy Carrier Trade-offs

The optimal energy carrier mix depends on:
- **Flight distance**: Shorter flights favor batteries, longer flights need hydrogen or SAF
- **Technology maturity**: Earlier deployment possible with conventional architecture
- **Resource availability**: Hydrogen and electrofuels compete for electricity
- **Grid carbon intensity**: Low-carbon electricity enables both e-fuels and electric/H2 aircraft

Key insight: Hydrogen and electricity preferentially allocated to alternative aircraft rather than electrofuel production when alternative aircraft are available and efficient.

## Scenario-Robust Mitigation

Robust scenarios optimize a single policy across multiple background scenarios (SSP2-1.9, SSP2-2.6, SSP2-3.4).

### Optimization Strategy

Rather than optimize for SSP2-2.6 alone, optimize for flexibility across a range of futures representing different:
- Electricity availability
- Grid carbon intensity  
- Radiative forcing targets

### Key Findings

1. **Trade-off Between Optimality and Robustness**:
   - Higher emissions in target scenario (SSP2-2.6) compared to single-scenario optimum
   - But better performance across scenario range
   - Value of robustness depends on scenario uncertainty

2. **Deployment Strategy**:
   - Size alternative aircraft deployment for worst-case scenario (SSP2-3.4)
   - Use surplus low-carbon electricity in better scenarios for electrofuels
   - Leaves flexibility for variability in electricity system

3. **Scenario-Dependent Patterns**:

   **Lower warming (SSP2-1.9)**:
   - Earlier electrofuel deployment (lower grid carbon intensity)
   - Higher electrofuel shares (higher electricity availability)
   - Higher FT biofuel shares
   - Higher overall biofuel in Jet-A blend

   **Higher warming (SSP2-3.4)**:
   - Less alternative aircraft (higher grid emissions, lower electricity)
   - More reliance on biofuels
   - Higher fossil kerosene consumption

4. **Policy Implications**:
   - Robust policies sacrifice some performance in expected scenario
   - Gain resilience to scenario uncertainty
   - Important for long-term infrastructure investments
   - Alternative aircraft deployment is key flexibility mechanism

## Comparison with AR6 Scenario Ensemble

The scenarios from this work are compared with the AR6 scenario database ensemble.

### Agreement Through Mid-Century

Through ~2050:
- Energy consumption: Within AR6 ensemble range
- CO2 emissions: Within AR6 ensemble range
- Validates consistency with multi-model ensemble
- Differences reflect scope variations (domestic vs international, freight, etc.)

### Divergence After 2050

Beyond 2050, this work projects lower energy and emissions:

**Reasons for Divergence**:
1. **Demand Saturation**: Explicit modeling of per-capita demand saturation at high incomes
2. **Fleet Renewal**: Full fleet turnover by 2070 captures efficiency gains
3. **Technology Detail**: Aircraft-specific performance rather than aggregated assumptions

**Interpretation**:
- Lower projections are plausible given demand saturation effects
- Consistent with historical patterns of transport mode maturation
- Highlights importance of modeling long-term behavioral changes
- Does not invalidate AR6 ensemble (different scopes and assumptions)

## Key Insights Across All Scenarios

1. **Timing is Critical**:
   - Emissions peak around 2040 across scenarios
   - Window for alternative aircraft deployment is 2030-2050
   - Late action significantly increases cumulative emissions

2. **Resource Constraints Matter**:
   - Limited biomass and low-carbon electricity are binding constraints
   - Fair share allocation crucial for multi-sector decarbonization
   - Competition between sectors for limited resources

3. **Technology Uncertainty is High**:
   - Factor of 2-3x variation in emissions between technology scenarios
   - Early technology choices have long-term lock-in effects
   - Hedging strategies valuable given uncertainty

4. **Complementarity of Measures**:
   - SAF alone insufficient for deep decarbonization
   - Alternative aircraft alone cannot meet ambitious targets without resource availability
   - Demand management required for Paris alignment with limited resources
   - Combined approach most effective

5. **Market Segmentation Crucial**:
   - Different markets require different solutions
   - Short-range benefits from alternative aircraft first
   - Long-range requires either mature hydrogen or drop-in fuels
   - Network effects not captured but likely important
